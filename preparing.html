The Journey to an IEP

This guide walks you through every step of the IEP evaluation process, from your initial request through the eligibility meeting. Understanding what to expect helps you feel prepared and empowered to advocate for your child.

Remember: You are not alone in this process. You are your child's best advocate, and you have important legal rights at every step.

Timeline Overview

From Request to IEP: Typical Timeline

  • Day 0: You submit written evaluation request
  • Days 1-15: School responds to your request
  • Days 15-30: You receive and sign consent forms
  • Days 30-90: Evaluation conducted (60 days from consent in most states)
  • Days 90-120: Eligibility meeting held (within 30 days of completing evaluation)
  • Days 120-150: If eligible, IEP developed and implemented

Note: Timelines vary by state. This is a general guideline.

Step 1: After You Submit Your Request

Photo by Scott Graham on Unsplash

What You Should Do

  • Keep a copy: Save a copy of your evaluation request letter for your records
  • Send via certified mail OR get receipt: If mailing, use certified mail with return receipt. If hand-delivering, ask for a date-stamped copy
  • Follow up: If you don't hear back within 10 business days, follow up with a phone call or email
  • Document everything: Keep a log of all communications with the school

What the School Will Do

  • Review your request: Special education staff will review your concerns
  • Gather existing data: They'll review your child's academic records, grades, and any previous assessments
  • Consult with teachers: They may speak with your child's teacher(s) about your concerns
  • Make a decision: The team decides whether to agree to evaluate or refuse

Possible Responses

If School Agrees to Evaluate

You will receive:

  • Prior Written Notice: Formal document explaining what evaluations they propose
  • Consent Forms: Forms for you to review and sign giving permission to evaluate
  • Notice of Procedural Safeguards: A document explaining your rights
  • Evaluation plan: Information about what assessments will be done

If School Refuses to Evaluate

You will receive:

  • Prior Written Notice: Document explaining why they're refusing
  • Notice of Procedural Safeguards: Your rights document

Your Options:

  • Request a meeting to discuss the decision
  • Request mediation
  • File a state complaint
  • Request a due process hearing
  • Get an independent evaluation (at your expense initially, may be reimbursed)

Step 2: Reviewing and Signing Consent Forms

What the Consent Forms Will Include

  • Areas to be assessed: What aspects of your child will be evaluated (cognitive, academic, behavioral, etc.)
  • Types of assessments: Specific tests and observations that will be used
  • Who will evaluate: School psychologist, speech therapist, OT, etc.
  • Your rights: Information about your ability to revoke consent

Important Things to Know

Read Carefully Before Signing

  • Make sure all your concerns are addressed: If you requested evaluation in reading but the consent only mentions speech, question it
  • Ask questions: If you don't understand something, ask for clarification
  • You can request additional assessments: If you think something is missing, ask for it to be added
  • Keep a copy: Always keep a signed copy for your records
  • You can revoke consent: You have the right to stop the evaluation at any time (though this may not be in your child's best interest)

What You Can Do to Help

  • Provide copies of any outside evaluations or medical records
  • Share information about your child's strengths and challenges
  • Complete any questionnaires the school sends you
  • Inform the school of any recent changes (medications, family situations, etc.)

Step 3: The Evaluation Period

Photo by Compare Fibre on Unsplash

What Will Happen

Your child will be assessed by various specialists over several sessions. This typically includes:

Observations

  • Specialists will observe your child in the classroom
  • They'll note how your child interacts with peers and teachers
  • They'll observe academic engagement and behaviors

One-on-One Assessments

  • Your child will be pulled out of class for testing sessions
  • Sessions typically last 30-60 minutes
  • Multiple sessions may be needed
  • Different specialists test different areas

Review of Existing Data

  • Evaluators review report cards, work samples, and previous assessments
  • They review teacher input and notes
  • They consider your input and concerns
  • They review any medical or outside evaluation information you provided

Common Assessment Types

Cognitive/IQ Testing

What it measures: Overall intellectual ability, processing speed, working memory, reasoning

Who does it: School psychologist

Common tests: WISC-V, WAIS-IV, Woodcock-Johnson

Academic Achievement

What it measures: Reading, writing, math skills compared to grade-level expectations

Who does it: School psychologist or special education teacher

Common tests: WIAT-III, Woodcock-Johnson Achievement

Social-Emotional/Behavioral

What it measures: Emotional functioning, behavior patterns, social skills

Who does it: School psychologist or counselor

Common tools: BASC-3, Conners, observations, rating scales (completed by parents and teachers)

Speech/Language

What it measures: Articulation, receptive/expressive language, pragmatic language

Who does it: Speech-language pathologist (SLP)

Common tests: CELF-5, Goldman-Fristoe, PPVT

Occupational Therapy (if needed)

What it measures: Fine motor skills, handwriting, sensory processing, visual-motor integration

Who does it: Occupational therapist (OT)

Common tests: Beery VMI, Bruininks-Oseretsky, sensory profile

What You Should Do During This Time

  • Stay in communication: Check in with the school about progress
  • Ask your child: Talk to your child about how the testing is going (without creating anxiety)
  • Keep notes: Document any new concerns or observations
  • Provide support: Ensure your child gets good sleep, eats well, and feels supported
  • Don't coach: Don't try to prepare your child for specific tests - this can invalidate results

If There Are Delays

The school has a legal timeline to complete the evaluation (typically 60 days from consent). If you're approaching the deadline:

  • Contact the school to ask about the status
  • Document the timeline in writing
  • If the deadline passes, send a written reminder citing the legal timeline
  • Consider filing a state complaint if there are significant delays without good reason

Step 4: Receiving the Evaluation Report

What You'll Receive

You should receive a comprehensive evaluation report that includes:

  • Background information: Your concerns, child's history, reason for referral
  • Assessment results: Detailed results from each test and observation
  • Interpretation: What the results mean
  • Strengths and weaknesses: Your child's profile
  • Summary and recommendations: Whether evaluators believe your child has a disability

Understanding Test Scores

Common Score Types

  • Standard Scores: Average is 100, with most people scoring between 85-115
  • Percentile Ranks: If your child scores in the 25th percentile, they performed better than 25% of same-age peers
  • Grade Equivalents: If your 4th grader scores at a 2nd grade level in reading, this indicates a 2-year delay
  • Age Equivalents: Similar to grade equivalents but based on age

Note: Scores alone don't determine eligibility - the team considers multiple factors.

What to Do When You Receive the Report

Review Carefully Before the Meeting

  1. Read it thoroughly: Take time to understand all sections
  2. Highlight questions: Mark anything you don't understand
  3. Note disagreements: If something doesn't seem right, note it
  4. Check for completeness: Were all your concerns addressed?
  5. Share with advocates: Give a copy to anyone helping you (attorney, advocate, etc.)
  6. Prepare questions: Write down questions to ask at the meeting

It's okay if you don't understand everything - the meeting is your opportunity to ask questions and get clarification.

Step 5: The Eligibility Meeting

Photo by Austin Distel on Unsplash

Who Will Be There

  • You (Parent/Guardian) - Equal member of the team
  • School Administrator - Often principal or assistant principal
  • Special Education Teacher - Expert in special education
  • General Education Teacher - Your child's current teacher
  • School Psychologist - Presents evaluation results
  • Other Specialists - SLP, OT, PT if they evaluated your child
  • Anyone You Bring - Advocate, attorney, spouse, friend, therapist, etc.

Meeting Agenda

The meeting typically follows this structure:

  1. Introductions (5 minutes)
    • Everyone introduces themselves and their role
  2. Purpose of Meeting (5 minutes)
    • Chairperson explains the purpose: to determine eligibility for special education
  3. Review of Evaluations (30-45 minutes)
    • Each evaluator presents their findings
    • Tests, scores, and observations are discussed
    • You can ask questions throughout
  4. Parent Input (10-15 minutes)
    • You share your observations and concerns
    • You can agree or disagree with findings
  5. Eligibility Determination (15-20 minutes)
    • Team discusses whether child meets criteria
    • Must have: (1) a qualifying disability AND (2) need for special education
  6. Next Steps (10 minutes)
    • If eligible: Schedule IEP development meeting
    • If not eligible: Discuss other options (504 plan, interventions, etc.)

Your Rights at the Meeting

You Have the Right To:

  • Participate equally: Your input has equal weight
  • Ask questions: Request clarification on anything
  • Disagree: You can disagree with findings or eligibility decision
  • Request independent evaluation: At school expense if you disagree
  • Take breaks: Ask for a break if needed
  • Bring support: Anyone you want can attend with you
  • Record the meeting: Audio recording (check state law)
  • Request another meeting: If you need more time

Tips for the Meeting

DO:

  • Arrive early and bring copies of everything
  • Take notes (or bring someone to take notes for you)
  • Ask for clarification on anything you don't understand
  • Share your perspective and observations
  • Stay calm and professional
  • Request a copy of all documents discussed

DON'T:

  • Sign anything without reading and understanding it
  • Let the team rush you into a decision
  • Agree with findings you don't believe are accurate
  • Leave without understanding the decision and next steps
  • Be afraid to ask questions - even "basic" ones

Step 6: After the Eligibility Decision

If Your Child is Found Eligible

What Happens Next

  1. IEP Meeting Scheduled: Within 30 days of eligibility determination
  2. IEP is Developed: Team creates the actual IEP document with goals, services, and accommodations
  3. Services Begin: Once you sign the IEP, services must begin as soon as possible
  4. Progress Monitoring: School reports on progress toward IEP goals (typically quarterly)
  5. Annual Review: IEP reviewed and updated at least once per year
  6. Re-evaluation: Child re-evaluated every 3 years (or sooner if requested)

Preparing for the IEP Development Meeting

  • Think about your goals for your child this school year
  • Consider what accommodations might help
  • Research what services might be appropriate
  • Prepare questions about placement and services
  • Bring any additional information about your child's needs

If Your Child is Found NOT Eligible

Your Options

  • Request Explanation: Make sure you understand why they found your child ineligible
  • Request Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You can request this at school expense if you disagree with their evaluation
  • Explore 504 Plan: Your child might qualify for accommodations under Section 504
  • Request RTI/MTSS Interventions: Regular education interventions that might help
  • File for Due Process: You can dispute the eligibility decision
  • Get Private Evaluation: Consider having your child evaluated privately
  • Re-request Evaluation Later: You can request evaluation again if concerns persist

Understanding Why

Common reasons for ineligibility:

  • Child's scores don't meet the criteria for any disability category
  • Child has a disability but doesn't need specialized instruction (may qualify for 504)
  • The gap between ability and achievement isn't significant enough
  • Difficulties are due to other factors (language barriers, attendance, lack of instruction)

What to Do If You Disagree

If you believe the decision is wrong:

  1. Request detailed explanation in writing
  2. Request Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE)
  3. Consult with a special education advocate or attorney
  4. Consider filing for mediation or due process
  5. Keep documenting your child's struggles
  6. Consider re-requesting evaluation after interventions are tried

How to Prepare: Checklist

Before the Eligibility Meeting

  • ☐ Read the evaluation report thoroughly
  • ☐ Highlight sections you have questions about
  • ☐ Write down your questions
  • ☐ Prepare your own observations about your child
  • ☐ Gather any additional information (medical records, outside evaluations, work samples)
  • ☐ Decide if you want to bring an advocate or supporter
  • ☐ Review your procedural safeguards (rights document)
  • ☐ Confirm meeting date, time, and location
  • ☐ Plan to arrive 10 minutes early
  • ☐ Bring notebook and pen for notes
  • ☐ Bring copies of evaluation report and any documents you want to share
  • ☐ Consider bringing audio recorder (if allowed in your state)
  • ☐ Prepare yourself emotionally - this can be hard to hear

Remember

You are your child's best advocate. The evaluation process can feel overwhelming, but you know your child better than anyone in that room. Your observations, your concerns, and your voice matter.

It's a process, not a single moment. Whether your child is found eligible or not, this is not the end of your advocacy journey. You have rights, you have options, and you have the ability to keep fighting for what your child needs.

You can do this. Thousands of parents successfully navigate this process every year. With preparation and knowledge of your rights, you can ensure your child gets the support they deserve.

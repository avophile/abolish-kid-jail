You Have the Right to Request an Evaluation

Under federal law, you have the right to request a free evaluation to determine if your child is eligible for special education services. This guide explains your rights, the evaluation process, and what happens next.

Key Point: The school must respond to your request and either conduct the evaluation or provide you with written explanation of why they are refusing - which is subject to your right to dispute their decision.

Your Rights Under IDEA

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) is a federal law that guarantees students with disabilities the right to a free appropriate public education (FAPE) in the least restrictive environment.

Right to Request an Evaluation

  • You can request at any time: Parents have the right to request an evaluation whenever they suspect their child may have a disability
  • Request must be in writing: While verbal requests are valid, written requests create a clear record and start the legal timeline
  • School must respond: The district must respond within a "reasonable time" (typically 10-15 business days in most states)
  • Evaluation is free: The school must conduct the evaluation at no cost to you
  • Evaluation must be comprehensive: Must assess all areas of suspected disability

What Happens After Your Request

Option 1: School Agrees to Evaluate

  • School sends you written notice of proposed evaluation
  • You receive consent forms to review and sign
  • Evaluation must be completed within 60 days (or state timeline)
  • Team meets to review results and determine eligibility

Option 2: School Refuses to Evaluate

  • School must provide written notice explaining refusal
  • You have the right to dispute this decision
  • Options include mediation, filing a state complaint, or due process hearing
  • Consider getting an independent evaluation

Parental Consent and Participation

  • Your consent is required: The school cannot evaluate without your written consent
  • You can participate: You have the right to provide input and information during the evaluation
  • You receive all results: You must receive a copy of the evaluation report
  • You are part of the team: You participate as an equal member of the IEP team

The Evaluation Process

What the School Must Do

A proper evaluation under IDEA must include:

1. Multiple Assessments

  • No single test or assessment can be used to determine eligibility
  • Assessments must be in all areas of suspected disability
  • Must use a variety of tools and strategies

2. Qualified Evaluators

  • Evaluations must be conducted by trained professionals
  • Different specialists assess different areas (psychologist, speech therapist, etc.)

3. Non-Discriminatory Assessment

  • Tests must be in the child's native language or mode of communication
  • Must be free from cultural or racial bias
  • Must be validated for the purpose they're being used

4. Functional and Developmental Information

  • Must assess how the disability affects academic performance
  • Must consider developmental, functional, and academic information
  • Must include input from parents

Common Evaluation Components

Academic Achievement

Testing in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas to determine current performance levels

Cognitive/Intellectual

Assessment of cognitive abilities, processing speed, working memory, and reasoning skills

Social-Emotional/Behavioral

Observation and assessment of behavior, emotional regulation, and social skills

Speech and Language

Assessment of articulation, language comprehension, expression, and pragmatic language

Occupational Therapy (OT)

Fine motor skills, sensory processing, and ability to perform daily tasks

Physical Therapy (PT)

Gross motor skills, mobility, and physical development

Timeline

  • Response to request: "Reasonable time" - typically 10-15 business days
  • Evaluation completion: 60 calendar days from receiving your consent (or your state's timeline)
  • IEP meeting: Within 30 days of determining eligibility

Eligibility for Special Education

To be eligible for an IEP, a child must meet two criteria:

  1. Have one or more of the 13 disability categories under IDEA
  2. Need special education services because of that disability

The 13 Disability Categories

1. Specific Learning Disability (SLD)

Most common category. Includes dyslexia, dyscalculia, dysgraphia. Affects ability to understand or use language, do math calculations, or coordinate movements.

2. Other Health Impairment (OHI)

Includes ADHD, chronic health conditions (asthma, diabetes, epilepsy, etc.) that limit strength, vitality, or alertness.

3. Autism Spectrum Disorder

Developmental disability affecting communication and social interaction.

4. Emotional Disturbance

Condition with characteristics including inability to learn not explained by other factors, relationship difficulties, inappropriate behaviors or feelings, depression or fears.

5. Speech or Language Impairment

Communication disorder such as stuttering, impaired articulation, language or voice impairment.

6. Intellectual Disability

Below-average intellectual functioning with deficits in adaptive behavior.

7. Hearing Impairment or Deafness

Hearing loss that affects educational performance.

8. Visual Impairment or Blindness

Vision problem that affects educational performance even with correction.

9. Orthopedic Impairment

Physical disability affecting education (cerebral palsy, amputations, etc.).

10. Multiple Disabilities

Combination of disabilities causing severe educational needs.

11. Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

Brain injury causing impairments affecting educational performance.

12. Deaf-Blindness

Combined hearing and visual impairments.

13. Developmental Delay

For children ages 3-9 (varies by state). Delay in physical, cognitive, communication, social, emotional, or adaptive development.

Important: Having a medical diagnosis does NOT automatically qualify a child for an IEP. The disability must impact their educational performance and they must need specialized instruction.

After the Evaluation: The IEP Meeting

Photo by Austin Distel on Unsplash

If Your Child is Found Eligible

The IEP team will develop an Individualized Education Program that includes:

1. Present Levels of Performance

  • How the disability affects academic performance
  • Current strengths and needs
  • Baseline data

2. Annual Goals

  • Measurable academic and functional goals
  • How progress will be measured
  • When progress will be reported

3. Special Education Services

  • Specialized instruction needed
  • Related services (speech therapy, OT, counseling, etc.)
  • Amount and frequency of services

4. Accommodations and Modifications

  • Classroom accommodations (extra time, preferential seating, etc.)
  • Testing accommodations
  • Curriculum modifications if needed

5. Placement

  • Where services will be provided (general education class, resource room, etc.)
  • How much time in general education vs. special education
  • Must be in least restrictive environment (LRE)

If Your Child is Found NOT Eligible

  • School must provide written notice explaining the decision
  • You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at school expense
  • You can dispute the decision through mediation or due process
  • Consider requesting a 504 Plan if child doesn't qualify for IEP but still needs support

Your Ongoing Rights

Procedural Safeguards

You have the right to:

  • Participate as equal member of IEP team
  • Receive notice of all meetings with enough time to attend
  • Bring advocates, attorneys, or other supporters to meetings
  • Request IEP meetings at any time
  • Request changes to the IEP at any time
  • Request re-evaluation (but school must evaluate at least every 3 years)
  • Access all educational records
  • Receive notice before school makes changes to services or placement
  • Dispute decisions through mediation or due process

Dispute Resolution

If you disagree with the school's decisions, you have several options:

Informal Resolution

Request a meeting to discuss concerns with teacher, school psychologist, or principal

Facilitated IEP Meeting

Request a neutral facilitator to help the IEP team communicate effectively

Mediation

Free, voluntary process with trained mediator to resolve disputes

State Complaint

File written complaint with state education agency alleging violation of IDEA

Due Process Hearing

Formal legal proceeding before impartial hearing officer (similar to court)

504 Plans vs. IEPs

504 Plan IEP
Law Section 504 of Rehabilitation Act Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)
Eligibility Has disability that substantially limits major life activity Has 1 of 13 disabilities AND needs special education

You Are Your Child's Best Advocate

Navigating the special education system can be overwhelming, but you are not alone. Millions of families successfully advocate for their children every year. Knowledge of your rights is the first step to ensuring your child receives the support they need to thrive.

Remember: You are an equal member of the IEP team. Your insights about your child are invaluable. Don't be afraid to ask questions, request clarification, or disagree when necessary. Your child deserves your advocacy.
